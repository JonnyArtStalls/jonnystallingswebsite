Sonification of Trapped Particles

Iannis Xenakis brought particles to life in his 1956 work Pithoprakta. The piece contained a sequence of glissandi modeled after the random walk of Brownian particles in a fluid. The sequence was unique because it converted an intrinsic aspect of stochastic motion, namely the chance variation in speed between particle collisions, to audible sound.

Inspired by Xenakis' work, I used a modern laser trapping setup to track Brownian particles and sonify their motion. I built the trapping setup as part of my master's thesis in Physics at Wake Forest University in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. I sonified the position data of single, optically-trapped Brownian microspheres (like the one shown at right) using Pure Data and Ableton Live. Each radial displacement of the sphere mapped to an audible pitch.

I used three different mapping schemes—equal-area, biased, and probability mapping. In each scheme, the centermost subregion of the mapping region corresponded to a single pure tone. In equal-area mapping, the note increased along a particular musical scale as the particle moved out radially into different equal-area subregions. In biased mapping, the centermost subregion area was enlarged relative to the other subregions. In probability mapping, the note had a 50-50 percent probability of increasing by some integer number of midi note numbers or decreasing by some other integer number of midi note numbers each time the particle entered a new subregion.

I wrote a conference paper that provides more details on the project. I also made a series of videos that synchronize the sounds to the actual experimental motion.
